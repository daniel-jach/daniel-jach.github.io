# Publications 

### Framework

I think of language as the network of trails that cut through the landscape surrounding my home village in southern Germany. When I arrived, they were already there. I learned to use them because they got me to the important places quickly and reliably. I was warned not to stray from the path but somebody had to explore alternative routes and discover hidden places. When I left, the network was still there, and some new connections had emerged. Language is just like that. Following research in usage-based linguistics, I assume that linguistic structures emerge from language use because they prove to be effective ways to communicate my perspectives and intentions to others. Recurring form-meaning patterns become entrenched in memory and develop into more and more schematic constructions.

### Theses  

`2019`

<a href="material/phd-thesis.pdf" target="_blank">PhD</a>

In my doctoral thesis, I compared the use of prepositional relative clauses in native and nonnative English. More specifically, I looked at the factors that determine the word order variation known as preposition placement (e.g., *the topic about which I talked* vs *which I talked about*). Multivariate regression analyses of corpus and experimental data revealed correlations of preposition placement and multiple usage-based variables such as frequency of exposure, collocational strength, cross-linguistic similarity, proficiency, and construction meaning. I argue that the two word orders are best understood as network of constructions which represent usage patterns at different levels of schematicity. The thesis is in English. 

`2014`

<a href="material/ma-thesis-jena.pdf" target="_blank">MA Jena</a>

My Jena master thesis reports a repetition experiment with Chinese learners of German and native controls. Participants repeated German sentences with subject and object relative clauses (e.g., *der Hund, der den Mann beißt* "the dog who bites the man" vs *der Hund, den der Mann beißt* "the dog who the man bites") of different form and meaning. Object relative clauses are generally considered more complex and therefore more difficult to learn and produce than subject relative clauses. Contrary to this, the results of an ANOVA indicated that prototypical object relative clauses were not more difficult to repeat than subject relative clauses, suggesting that learning and producing depend more on usage experience than on complexity. The thesis is in German.

`2011`

<a href="material/ma-thesis-knoxville.pdf" target="_blank">MA Knoxville</a>

My Knoxville master thesis discusses various approaches to text types using the example of Rainald Götz' German-language novel *Loslabern*. The analysis suggests that the text is best understood as inter-text whose comprehension requires knowledge of similar texts which are linked in a network of family resemblance (Wittgenstein). Similarity is described on multiple levels from grammatical form to communicative function and situation. The thesis is in German.

`2010`

<a href="material/ba-thesis.pdf" target="_blank">BA</a>

In my bachelor thesis, I analyze the German-language construction "possessive dative" (e.g., *meinem Vater sein Hut* "my father his hat") which is common only in spoken non-standard language use. I argue that the construction survives in spoken language use because of its pragmatic function of anchoring unknown referents in common ground. The thesis is in German.
